Tunes, Riffs, and Licks

Otto (they/them) is a Jewish, nonbinary poet from Jersey City. We’ve known each other for more than ten years and stayed in touch over that time. Otto is one of the few students who continued writing, performing, and teaching poetry after high school. As a teaching-artist, they have worked with various nonprofits, youth arts organizations, and public school districts. In my earlier work (Mooney, 2016), I discussed how Otto explored gender and masculinity through poetry and how they helped me come to a more nuanced understanding of my own gender identity. In this way, Otto’s presence in this project reaffirms a key aim of the webtext: to center youth literacies while reshaping how we understand knowledge production through a nonbinary spectrum of modalities.

As we reflected back on our experiences together, Otto recalled memories with poetry as (trans)formative experiences that shaped them into the person they are today. Our interviews were full of joy and laughter and poems. We read old and new work to one another, picking up almost right where we left off before Otto went away to college. As we began to think about composing a song together, many meaningful parts of Otto’s identity emerged, including their love of video games, technology, and music. We discussed the stunning and imaginative world-building of games like Destiny and Zelda—not so different from the world-building in poems or musical compositions. The original soundtracks (OSTs) of video games like Destiny became a generative source of samples for “Otto’s Song.”

Another hobby that Otto took up in recent years is making custom computer keyboards. We decided to sample the sound of Otto pressing buttons on one of these keyboards as a texture for the track. This sample became a layer of percussion in the final composition. During high school, Otto played guitar as a music major in our high school’s performing arts department. I asked if they had any old recordings we could sample for the song. Otto told me they still had “all these [unfinished] tunes and riffs and licks.” After a little searching, we found an old recording of Otto playing guitar, which makes up one of the melodic leads for the song we composed together. This blend of guitar riffs, keyboard sounds, and game aesthetics functions as both sonic data and symbolic texture, a personalized soundscape that embodies Otto’s layered intersectional identities.

Decolonized Judaism

During our interviews, Otto read poems about Jewish identity. They told me about how they’ve “been thinking a lot about my Jewish identity in terms of queerness and anticolonialism” and over the years have been contemplating a “decolonized Judaism.” Otto criticized the occupation of Palestine and distinguished between Judaism and Israeli statehood, reflecting on what makes something a home(land), which reminded me of conversations with Edsel. As a person and artist committed to social justice, Otto demonstrated a sensitivity to the humanity of all people, including their Jewish ancestors and the trans community.

In many respects, Otto’s poetry speaks for itself. There is always so much to say—and yet, to be left unsaid. Some of Otto’s newer poems, which explore their identity as a descendant of the Holocaust, leave space for the unsaid / the erased / the ghosts between the words / honoring that absence through silence. They remembered how “first there was a people / then there was smoke,” and then as we move through generations, “what the fire left / was me.” Otto talked about the impact of “generational trauma” and “historical memory,” which resonated from my conversations with Aaleah.

Queering the Soundscape

One summer when Otto was home from college, we got together to catch up. Earlier that year, one of Otto’s friends at school, a trans woman (they/them), took their own life. I remember hearing the grief in Otto’s voice and trying to find the right words to console and comfort. It wasn’t until recently that I discovered the poem Otto wrote in dedication to this friend, which asks “you think I want to archive / my friend into memoria / just to keep them alive?” That poem, sampled here in the song, speaks to the ways Otto uses poetry to make sense of an “inheritance I’m sorting through.” If inheritance is something given to us from the past, how do we make sense of it in the present and what is its role in our futures? What do we do with an inheritance of loss? And what does inheritance sound like when we claim it in our writing and creative work?

Although many of the poems Otto shared with me document trauma and loss, there is also a very present and intentional joy in their work. In the poem referenced above about the loss of their friend, Otto writes, “but in this space / I feel like I’m tending a garden / I feel joy / I grow flowers.” This joy was present in our interviews when we reflected on our time together. At one point, we found ourselves laughing uncontrollably, struggling to catch our breath after Otto made a joke about Neo from The Matrix, whom the Wachowski sisters (both trans women who produced the film) have described as a metaphor for the trans experience. And it is this sound of laughter that lingers, persists, and remains with me more than any other.

Poem as Exhibit

As Otto and I walked through these experiences together, it felt almost like visiting a museum that had different rooms and galleries from our past. We began to talk about remixing those experiences into something new. Otto described our collaborative process as “remixing the random schema of our experiences” and “splicing, flipping, or remixing” our memories. The very act of conjuring memory and re-membering constitutes a certain kind of remix. When I asked Otto how they understood poetry now, they talked about the “poem as exhibit,” as something you curate, and wondered aloud what happens if your reader or listener “walks through it in a different order than you curated?” They went on to suggest, “then you have a new thing.” I thought about my own research as a kind of exhibit that is curated for those who visit the site—and how an album format supports a certain form of agency for the reader/listener because they can choose to walk through it in a different order than the track list suggests. Put it on shuffle! Listen on repeat! Start at the end and finish at the beginning! Albums, mixtapes, and playlists have a specific order, but this order can be altered to provide a personalized modular experience.

Each of the songs I created with my collaborators, including Otto’s, became an exhibit of artifacts. The words are disjointed, out of context, jumbled, colliding, and overlapping—yet thematically organized and tied together in one cohesive exhibit. Otto believes that “where beauty or new insight happens is when you find proximity of two concepts that were once distant.” Each of these compositions puts words from different points of origin into spatio-temporal proximity, thereby creating new meanings that didn’t exist before. In essence, this is what a poem does. Otto deepened my thinking about this idea by using the language of music to describe poetry. They recalled how “theme, variation, and repetition” create complexity over time. These devices connect music and poetry in fundamental ways. Just as Otto described the “poem as exhibit,” this webtext functions as a nonlinear digital gallery that invites readers to curate their own experience as active participants to the site.
